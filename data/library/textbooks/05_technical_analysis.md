# Technical Analysis

## What Technical Analysis Is

Technical analysis (TA) studies historical price and volume data to identify patterns that may predict future movements. It rests on the premise that market behavior repeats because collective psychology is consistent.

TA is probabilistic. It deals in likelihoods, not certainties. Treat every signal as a hypothesis, not a directive.

## Candlestick Charts

Each candlestick represents price action over a fixed time period (1 minute, 1 hour, 1 day, etc.).

- **Body:** The range between open and close. Green (or white) = close above open (price rose). Red (or black) = close below open (price fell).
- **Wicks (shadows):** The thin lines above and below the body. They show the high and low extremes reached during the period.

Long wicks indicate rejection -- the price reached that level but was pushed back. Context matters -- the same pattern means different things at different price levels.

## Moving Averages

Moving averages smooth out price noise to reveal the underlying trend direction.

**Simple Moving Average (SMA):** Equal weight to all data points in the window. The 200-day SMA is widely watched as a long-term trend indicator.

**Exponential Moving Average (EMA):** Gives more weight to recent data. More responsive to current price action but also more prone to false signals.

Common configurations:
- **20-period:** Short-term trend
- **50-period:** Medium-term trend
- **200-period:** Long-term trend

**Golden cross** (short MA crosses above long MA) is bullish. **Death cross** is the reverse. Both are lagging -- by the time the cross occurs, much of the move has happened.

## RSI (Relative Strength Index)

RSI measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes on a 0-100 scale.

- **Above 70:** Conventionally "overbought." The asset has risen sharply and may pull back.
- **Below 30:** Conventionally "oversold." The asset has fallen sharply and may bounce.

Critical caveat: assets can remain overbought or oversold for extended periods, especially during strong trends. RSI divergence (price makes a new high but RSI doesn't) is often more useful than absolute RSI levels.

## MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)

MACD plots the difference between two EMAs (typically 12 and 26 period) and a signal line (9-period EMA of the MACD line).

- **MACD crosses above signal line:** Bullish momentum shift.
- **MACD crosses below signal line:** Bearish momentum shift.
- **Histogram:** Visualizes the gap between MACD and signal line. Growing histogram = strengthening momentum.

MACD is a trend-confirmation tool. It tells you whether momentum is accelerating or decelerating. It is not a timing tool for entries and exits.

## Bollinger Bands

A 20-period moving average with bands plotted at +/- 2 standard deviations.

- **Price near upper band:** High relative to recent history. Not necessarily a sell signal -- in strong uptrends, price rides the upper band.
- **Price near lower band:** Low relative to recent history. Same caveat applies.
- **Band width:** Narrow bands (squeeze) indicate low volatility, often preceding a large move. Wide bands indicate high volatility.

Bollinger Bands measure volatility and relative price levels, not direction.

## Volume Analysis

Volume is the most underrated confirmation tool.

- **Price up + volume up:** Strong buying conviction. Move is more likely to sustain.
- **Price up + volume down:** Weak move. Fewer participants driving the price higher. Vulnerable to reversal.
- **Price down + volume up:** Strong selling pressure. Likely to continue.
- **Price down + volume down:** Selling exhaustion. May indicate a bottom forming.

Always check volume when evaluating a price move. Price without volume is noise.

## Support and Resistance

**Support:** A price level where buying interest has historically prevented further decline. **Resistance:** Where selling pressure has prevented further advance.

These are zones, not precise lines. They hold until they don't. When support breaks, it often becomes resistance, and vice versa.

## Timeframe Selection

The same asset looks different depending on the timeframe you analyze:

- **1-minute / 5-minute:** Dominated by noise. Useful only for very short-term execution.
- **1-hour / 4-hour:** Balances detail with signal. Common for active trading.
- **Daily / Weekly:** Shows clear trends with minimal noise. Best for higher-timeframe bias.

Your trading strategy determines your primary timeframe. Shorter strategies need shorter charts. Always check at least one higher timeframe for context.

## The Limitations

Technical analysis is not predictive in any deterministic sense. Patterns fail regularly. Indicators lag by design -- they describe what has happened, not what will happen. In crypto specifically, a single piece of fundamental news (a hack, a regulatory ruling, an exchange collapse) can invalidate any technical setup instantly.

TA is one tool among many. Overreliance on any single indicator or pattern is a recipe for losses. Use TA to inform decisions, not to make them for you.
