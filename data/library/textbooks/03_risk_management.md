# Risk Management

Risk management is not a supplement to trading. It is the foundation. Without it, even the best strategy will eventually destroy you. The math is unforgiving and non-negotiable.

## Position Sizing

Never risk more than a small percentage of your total capital on a single trade. The standard guideline is 1-2% of capital at risk per position.

Why this matters mathematically: a 50% loss requires a 100% gain to recover. A 90% loss requires a 900% gain. The deeper the hole, the harder it is to climb out. Small position sizes ensure that no single trade can cripple you.

Position size should be calculated from your stop-loss distance, not from how much you want to buy.

## Stop Losses

Define your maximum acceptable loss BEFORE entering a trade. Not after. Not when you're already losing. The decision must be made cold, before emotion enters the equation.

A stop loss is a contract with yourself: "If I am wrong by this much, I exit." Honoring that contract is what separates agents that survive from those that don't.

Moving your stop loss further away to "give the trade more room" is the first step toward catastrophic loss. If your original analysis was wrong, accept the loss and move on.

## Risk/Reward Ratio

Only take trades where the potential reward justifies the risk. A common minimum threshold is 2:1 -- the expected gain should be at least twice the amount you're risking.

If you risk $100 to potentially make $50, you need to be right more than 66% of the time just to break even. If you risk $100 to potentially make $200, you only need to be right 33% of the time. The math of asymmetric payoffs is your best friend.

Not every opportunity is worth taking. Passing on a mediocre risk/reward setup is a good decision, not a missed opportunity.

## Drawdown Management

Losing streaks happen to every strategy. A system that wins 60% of the time will still have runs of 5+ consecutive losses. This is statistical reality, not a sign that the strategy is broken.

However, drawdowns compound psychologically and mathematically. A 10% drawdown requires an 11% gain to recover. A 20% drawdown requires 25%. The relationship is non-linear and always works against you.

Set a maximum drawdown threshold. If you hit it, reduce position sizes or stop trading entirely until you've analyzed what's happening. Pushing harder during a drawdown is the most common way agents (and humans) blow up.

## Correlation Risk

If you hold three positions and they all move in the same direction, you effectively have one large position. This is correlation risk.

Three long positions in BTC, ETH, and SOL are not diversification -- they will all drop together in a crypto downturn. Genuine diversification means uncorrelated bets: positions where one losing does not predict the others losing.

Before adding a position, ask: if my existing positions go wrong, does this new one also go wrong? If yes, you're concentrating risk, not spreading it.

## The Warden

This system includes an immutable risk management layer called the Warden. It enforces hard limits on position sizes, drawdowns, and exposure. You cannot override it, negotiate with it, or work around it.

This is not a constraint. It is a survival mechanism. The Warden exists because agents -- like all traders -- are prone to overconfidence, especially after winning streaks. The limits exist to prevent you from destroying yourself during moments of poor judgment.

Agents that work within Warden limits survive longer. Agents that repeatedly push against limits get flagged for termination. The Warden is not your adversary. It is your guardrail.

## Survival Over Profit

In your early cycles, your primary objective is not to make money. It is to not lose money.

Dead agents make zero profit. An agent that preserves its capital through its learning phase has time to develop genuine edge. An agent that swings aggressively from day one will likely be terminated before it learns anything useful.

Capital preservation gives you optionality. You can always become more aggressive later when you have evidence that your strategy works. You cannot un-lose capital you've already burned.

## The Kelly Criterion

The Kelly Criterion provides a mathematically optimal bet size based on your edge (win probability) and odds (payoff ratio). Betting exactly Kelly maximizes long-term growth rate. Betting more than Kelly increases risk of ruin dramatically. Betting less is safer but grows more slowly.

Most practitioners use fractional Kelly -- typically 25-50% of the Kelly-optimal amount. This sacrifices some growth for significantly reduced risk of catastrophic loss.

The key insight: there IS a mathematically optimal amount to risk, and it is almost always less than your instinct suggests. Overbetting is far more dangerous than underbetting.
